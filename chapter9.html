Chapter 9: Separating God from Harmful Voices

Rebuilding trust through clarity and truth

When harm has been experienced in the name of God, the impact does not remain isolated to the moment.

The association forms quickly. Words spoken by people become connected to God Himself.

Over time, confusion develops. Trust becomes difficult.

Real Situation

A person reflects on past experiences where words were spoken with authority and certainty.

Those words caused confusion, pressure, or distance. The name of God was attached to those moments.

Now, when hearing anything connected to God, hesitation appears immediately.

What This Reveals

The issue is not only what was said. The issue is what those words became associated with.

Harmful voices were not separated from God.

The Necessary Separation

Not every voice that used God’s name represented Him.

Not every experience reflects His nature.

Separation is required for clarity to return.

God is not defined by the voices that misrepresented Him.
Rebuilding Trust

Trust is not restored by ignoring past experiences. It is restored by re-evaluating them with clarity.

Step 1: Identify the Source

Was this truly the voice of Jesus, or was it a human voice using His name.

Step 2: Evaluate the Outcome

Did the result produce clarity, direction, and dignity, or confusion and distance.

Step 3: Separate the Two

What was human must not be attributed to God.

Step 4: Reintroduce Truth

Allow the voice of Jesus to be recognized again, apart from past distortion.

What Changes

As separation becomes clear, confusion decreases.

Trust begins to return, not as blind acceptance, but as informed recognition.

The voice of Jesus becomes distinguishable again.

Trust is not rebuilt by ignoring the past. Trust is rebuilt by understanding it correctly.
Scripture Framing

Jeremiah 23:16 reveals that voices can speak from their own heart while claiming God.

This explains why separation is necessary.

Final Observation

Separation allows clarity. Clarity allows trust.

The voice of Jesus was never the source of confusion. It was the misrepresentation that created it.

When that distinction is made, restoration becomes possible.
