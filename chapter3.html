Chapter 3: When Words Come from the Heart, Not God

Learning to recognize the difference

Not every sincere word comes from God. Not every confident voice carries truth.

Discernment begins when a person realizes that intention and origin are not the same thing.

A message can be delivered with care, passion, and confidence, yet still come from human perspective rather than the voice of Jesus.

Real Situation

A conversation feels meaningful and deep. The person speaking seems genuine and invested. Their words connect emotionally and appear thoughtful.

Advice is given. Direction is suggested. The moment feels significant.

Later, confusion sets in. The direction does not hold. The clarity fades. The words do not carry forward.

What This Reveals

The words came from sincerity, not from truth. Emotional connection created agreement, but no foundation was established.

Discernment requires looking beyond how something feels and examining what it produces over time.

Discernment Breakdown

Words from Human Source

Driven by emotion or experience

Sound right in the moment

Centered on immediate comfort

Lack lasting clarity

Shift depending on situation

Words from Jesus

Rooted in truth

Remain consistent over time

Bring clarity, not confusion

Carry direction

Produce growth and understanding

Discernment is not about rejecting people. Discernment is about recognizing source.
Scripture Framing

Jeremiah 23:16 identifies voices that speak from their own heart rather than from God.

This distinction explains why some words feel meaningful but fail to hold truth.

A Practical Shift

Discernment develops when attention shifts from:

How did this feel in the moment

To:

What is this producing in my life

Words that come from Jesus will continue to speak beyond the moment they were heard.

Clarity Marker

Confusion is not a stable outcome of truth. Lack of direction is not a sign of God’s voice.

Even when correction is present, clarity remains.

The voice of Jesus brings clarity that remains. Not intensity that fades.
Final Observation

Discernment is not immediate perfection. It develops through attention, reflection, and a willingness to question what once felt certain.

The goal is not to become skeptical of everything. The goal is to become anchored in truth.
